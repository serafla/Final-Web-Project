Subaru Cherry Blossom Festival

About

A project of the Japan America Society of Greater Philadelphia, the Subaru Cherry Blossom Festival is an annual celebration not only of spring and beautiful blossoms, but of Japanese culture as well. The Cherry Blossom Festival takes its name and central inspiration from the fleeting beauty of the sakura, the Japanese name for cherry blossoms. Each year, over the course of nine days, the events of the Festival celebrate Philadelphia’s rich cultural connections with the art, music, food, natural beauty, and industry of Japan.

History

The Subaru Cherry Blossom Festival has been a part of Philadelphia since 1997. Initially, the festival focused on the tree planting efforts of the Japan America Society of Greater Philadelphia. Echoing a sesquicentennial gift of 1,600 flowering trees made by Japan to Philadelphia in 1926, JASGP pledged to plant 1,000 cherry trees in Fairmount Park. This goal was accomplished in 2007, and the mission has expanded to plant cherry trees in community parks throughout the region.

As the number of cherry trees increased, so did Philadelphians’ interest in Japan. The Festival expanded to include an array of activities, from sushi making workshops and cultural demonstrations to music and dance performances. Together with the Philadelphia Flower Show, the Subaru Cherry Blossom Festival has become synonymous with the return of spring to Greater Philadelphia.

Location

Fairmount Park Horticulture Center

100 N. Horticultural Drive, Philadelphia, PA 19131

The Fairmount Park Horticulture Center is accessible via the Philadelphia PHLASH Downtown Loop. Riders can use the Please Touch Museum stop, which is just a three-minute walk from the festivities.

Events

The Subaru Cherry Blossom Festival is a two-day festival taking place at West Fairmount Park’s Horticulture Center. Featuring two performance stages, dozens of vendors, and activities ranging from origami to tea demonstrations to a Prettiest Pet in Pink contest, there’s something for the whole family.
